How to tell if you're really paying more for groceries

The headline inflation figure is an average across thousands of products and every household in the country. Your cart is not average. Cart Creep is a grocery inflation calculator built around the specific items you actually buy: log what you paid for the same short list each month and it works out your own inflation rate, then draws it right next to the government's food-at-home CPI. When your line climbs faster than the official one, you can see how much faster, and which items are driving it.

Questions people ask

What is a grocery inflation calculator?
It turns the prices you pay into a rate of change. Cart Creep sums your tracked items each month, indexes that total to your first month, and reports how far it has moved since. That is your grocery inflation rate, measured from real receipts instead of a national average.
Am I paying more for groceries than last year?
Log the same items across the months you want to compare and Cart Creep answers it directly. The chart plots your cumulative change from your starting month, so a point twelve months out shows exactly how much more (or less) the same cart costs than it did a year ago.
How is a personal inflation rate different from the CPI?
The CPI food-at-home index tracks a fixed national basket. Your personal inflation rate tracks your basket: if you buy a lot of coffee and eggs and those jumped, your rate runs hotter than the CPI in the same month. Cart Creep plots both on one axis from the same base month so the gap is easy to read.
How much data do I need before it means anything?
Two months of prices for at least one item is enough to draw your first trend line. Log every item each month for the most accurate comparison; a month with only some items priced will look cheaper than it really was.
Is my data private?
Yes. Everything stays in your browser's localStorage. There is no account, no server, and nothing you type is ever sent anywhere. Export your history to a JSON file whenever you want a backup, and import it back later.
